### P001 - Foshay et al. (2015)
**Title**: A Comprehensive Diagnostic Framework for Evaluating Business Intelligence and Analytics Effectiveness
**Journal**: Australasian Journal of Information Systems, Vol. 19, pp. S37-S54
**Authors**: Neil Foshay, William Yeoh, Yee-Ling Boo, Kok-Leong Ong, David Mattie
**Status**: ✅ Completed
**Date Read**: May 5, 2026

#### Keywords
**Primary Keywords**: BIA, Business Intelligence, Analytics, Diagnostic Framework, Maturity Model, Effectiveness Assessment, Design Science

**Secondary Keywords**:
- **Framework**: BIAED, Assessment Framework, Diagnostic Framework, Evaluation Framework
- **Assessment**: Capability Assessment, Maturity Assessment, Effectiveness Evaluation, Organizational Assessment
- **Stakeholders**: Constituencies, Stakeholder Analysis, Multi-stakeholder, User Perspectives, Technical Team, Senior Management
- **Process**: Process Context, Strategy Development, Iterative Process, Business-IT Alignment
- **Data**: Data Quality, Metadata, Data Governance, Data Architecture, Master Data Management
- **Culture**: Organizational Culture, Information-oriented Culture, Management Support, Culture Change
- **Methodology**: Design Science, Case Study, Mixed Methods, Online Surveys, Semi-structured Interviews
- **Tools**: BI Tools, Dashboard, Scorecard, Reports, ETL, MDM
- **Outcomes**: Strategy Formulation, Business Case, Investment Decision, Capability Evolution

**Search Tags**: #BIA #DiagnosticFramework #MaturityModel #DesignScience #StakeholderAnalysis #StrategyDevelopment #DataQuality #OrganizationalCulture

#### Key Concepts
- **BIAED Framework**: Business Intelligence and Analytics Effectiveness Diagnostic framework
- **Three Components**: Assessment dimensions, process context, constituencies
- **Management Levers**: Technical characteristics and facilitating conditions

#### Main Findings
1. BIA initiatives are costly, complex, and have high failure rates
2. Global BIA-related software spending exceeds US$14 Billion
3. Existing maturity models provide insufficient depth for ongoing evaluation
4. Multiple-stakeholder perspective yields valuable insights
5. Qualitative data (respondent commentary) is very useful for strategy development

#### Methodology
- **Research Design**: Design science paradigm (Hevner et al., 2004)
- **Participants**: 1500+ across 3 North American organizations
- **Organizations**:
  - International automobile manufacturer: 1050 participants
  - Halifax Regional Municipality: 300 participants
  - Property Valuation Services Corporation (PVSC): 150 participants
- **Data Collection**: Online surveys and semi-structured interviews

#### Assessment Dimensions
**Technical Characteristics**:
- Data Quality (accuracy, completeness, clarity, relevance)
- Metadata Quality (definitional, quality, lineage, navigational)
- Tools and Applications (reports, models, dashboards, scorecards)
- Technical Infrastructure (data architecture, system architecture, ETL, MDM)

**Facilitating Conditions**:
- Organizational Culture and Management Support
- Organizational Alignment and Process Integration
- BIA Practices/Development Approach
- BIA-related Skills and Knowledge
- Data Governance
- Project Governance

#### Constituencies (Stakeholder Groups)
1. **BIA Users**: Consumers of information resources, decision makers
2. **Technical Team**: Architecture, design, delivery, ongoing support
3. **Senior Management**: Funding, culture establishment

#### Process Context
Four-step iterative process:
1. BIA Effectiveness Assessment
2. Goal Setting and Gap Analysis
3. Strategy Development and Initiative Prioritization
4. Initiative Execution

#### Citations Available
- Chen et al. (2012) - BIA definition
- Gartner (2014) - BIA spending statistics
- Hevner et al. (2004) - Design science guidelines
- Thamir and Theodoulidis (2013) - BIA maturity model classification
- 25 existing BIA maturity models reviewed

### P002 - Bhuiyan (2026)
**Title**: Understanding Higher Education Institutional Cybersecurity Readiness Through Individual Mental Models of Privacy and Organisational Culture
**Institution**: Federation University Australia
**Authors**: Md Yeahia Bhuiyan
**Status**: ✅ Completed
**Date Read**: May 7, 2026

#### Keywords
**Primary Keywords**: Cybersecurity Readiness, Mental Models, Organisational Culture, Higher Education, Privacy, Protection Motivation Theory (PMT).

**Secondary Keywords**:
- **Framework**: Integrated Conceptual Framework, Behavioral Diagnostic Framework, Readiness Checklist.
- **Mental Models**: Cognitive Shortcuts, Privacy Mindsets, 13 Characteristics, Behavioral Intention.
- **Culture**: Schein's Model, Artefacts, Espoused Values, Underlying Assumptions, Leadership Signals.
- **HEI Context**: Academic Freedom, Decentralised Governance, Open-Access Systems, Policy Fatigue.
- **Methodology**: Explanatory Single Case Study, Template Analysis, NVivo, Expert Validation.

**Search Tags**: #CybersecurityReadiness #MentalModels #OrganisationalCulture #HigherEducation #Privacy #PMT #Schein #TemplateAnalysis

#### Key Concepts
- **Cybersecurity Readiness**: Behavioral, cultural, and governance capacity to anticipate and respond to risk.
- **Mental Model of Privacy**: Internal representation of data privacy and risk.
- **Integrated Framework**: Collaboration between PMT (individual) and Schein (organizational) layers.

#### Main Findings
1. Readiness emerges from the alignment of behavioral reasoning, cultural reinforcement, and governance clarity.
2. Individual intentions (PMT) only stabilize into secure habits when supported by culture and leadership.
3. High workload and cognitive demand are major barriers to cybersecurity compliance.
4. Identification of 13 specific mental model characteristics that filter policy interpretation.

#### Methodology
- **Research Design**: Explanatory single case study across 3 sequential phases.
- **Participants**: Students, academic staff, senior leaders, and experts at an Australian regional university.
- **Data Collection**: 22 Semi-structured interviews.
- **Validation**: Phase 3 expert panel validation.

#### Connections to Other Papers
- **P001 (Foshay et al., 2015)**: Both identify "Organizational Culture" as a critical facilitating condition for framework effectiveness. Both use semi-structured interviews to extract qualitative insights for strategy development.

#### Key Quotes
> "Cybersecurity readiness is a collective condition... it emerges when individual privacy reasoning is reinforced by visible leadership, clear accountability, usable processes, and realistic expectations."

> "Secure behavior becomes reliable only when it is supported as normal work, not treated as an additional demand."

#### Research Questions Addressed
- How do individual mental models of privacy impact cybersecurity behavior?
- How do organizational culture and behavioral intentions impact HEI cybersecurity practice?
- How do they influence overall organizational readiness?

#### Limitations
- Single case study (theoretical transferability, not statistical generalization).
- Reliance on self-reported data.
- Theoretical focus limited to PMT and Schein's model.

#### Future Directions
- Multi-case comparative studies.
- Longitudinal tracking of behavior via system logs.
- Integration of Institutional Theory or Socio-Technical perspectives.
