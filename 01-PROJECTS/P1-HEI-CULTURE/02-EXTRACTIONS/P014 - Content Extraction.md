## 🔬 Section-by-Section Details

### 1. Introduction
- **Problem**: Despite technical expertise, female CIOs often struggle to gain the same compliance levels as male counterparts.
- **Context**: The "Glass Ceiling" and "Sticky Floor" effects in cybersecurity leadership.

### 2. Theoretical Framework
- **Role Congruity Theory**: Proposing that women are stereotypically expected to be communal/transformational, while IT security is viewed as agentic/transactional.
- **Psychological Reactance**: The negative reaction when users feel their freedom is being restricted by an "incongruent" leader.

### 3. Methodology
- **Participants**: Diverse group of employees (controlled experiment).
- **Vignettes**: Scenarios describing a security policy rollout by a CIO (Male vs. Female, Transformational vs. Transactional style).

### 4. Key Findings
- **Finding 1**: Male CIOs can use Transactional (punishment-based) styles without significant reactance.
- **Finding 2**: Female CIOs face significant resistance (reactance) when using Transactional styles, but high compliance with Transformational styles.
- **Finding 3**: Perceived expertise partially moderates the gender effect but does not eliminate it.

### 5. Discussion & Strategic Implications
- **HEI Relevance**: University leadership must be aware of these biases. If a female CISO is rolling out a "Hard" mandate (like P010's mandated trust), the communication style must be carefully managed to avoid cultural reactance.
