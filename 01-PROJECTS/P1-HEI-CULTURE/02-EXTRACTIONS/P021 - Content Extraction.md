## 🔬 Section-by-Section Details

### 1. Introduction
- **Context**: Cybersecurity is increasingly recognized as an organizational rather than purely technical challenge.
- **Problem**: Lack of empirical evidence linking specific cultural types to readiness levels.

### 2. Theoretical Development
- **Schein's Layers**: Analyzing artifacts, espoused values, and basic underlying assumptions in the context of security.
- **The Competing Values Framework (CVF)**: Categorizing cultures into Clan, Adhocracy, Hierarchy, and Market.

### 3. Methodology
- **Target**: IT and non-IT staff at various large-scale organizations (including HEIs).
- **Instrument**: OCAI survey combined with a Cybersecurity Readiness assessment tool.

### 4. Key Findings
- **Discovery 1**: "Hierarchy" and "Market" cultures demonstrate the highest readiness levels due to formal processes and accountability.
- **Discovery 2**: "Clan" cultures (common in HEIs) show the lowest readiness, as high internal trust reduces perceived threat severity (PMT).
- **Discovery 3**: Organizational culture moderates the relationship between leadership actions (P019) and actual compliance.

### 5. Discussion & Strategic Implications
- **Strategic Recommendation**: HEI leaders must recognize their "Clan" culture and introduce "Hierarchical" security controls (like P008's policy mandates) without destroying the collaborative spirit.
- **Relevance for Project 1**: Supports the inclusion of **Cultural Type** as a primary diagnostic indicator.
