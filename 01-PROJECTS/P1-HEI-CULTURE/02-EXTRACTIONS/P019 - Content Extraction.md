## 🔬 Section-by-Section Details

### 1. Introduction
- **Problem**: HEIs face a growing volume of cyber-attacks, but leadership often remains detached from the security mission.
- **Core Argument**: Human error is a leadership failure.

### 2. The Unique HEI Landscape
- **Decentralization**: The struggle of centralized security in a decentralized academic environment.
- **Academic Freedom**: The tension between restrictive security policies and the need for open research and collaboration.

### 3. The Cybersecurity Leadership Framework
- **Pillar 1: Policy**: Creating clear, enforceable, and usable (P015) policies.
- **Pillar 2: Training & Awareness**: Moving beyond "Tick-Box" training to meaningful behavioral change (P009).
- **Pillar 3: Resource Allocation**: Ensuring security teams have the budget and personnel needed for proactive defense (P016).
- **Pillar 4: Accountability**: Establishing clear roles and consequences for non-compliance (P008).

### 4. Discussion: The Leader as a Role Model
- **Concept**: Secure behavior must start at the top. If executives bypass MFA, faculty will too (P012).

### 5. Conclusion
- **Implication for Project 1**: Leadership is the "Top Layer" of the readiness framework. Without it, individual mental models (P002) will remain unaligned with organizational goals.
